How may Secondhand Clothes help Fashion Lovers Protect the Environment in Hong Kong?

Reported by Liu Xiaoqian Lisa
LiuXiaoqian_Lisa · How may Secondhand Clothes help Fashion Lovers Protect The Environment in Hong Kong?

Samathy Woo used to buy clothes almost every week when working at an investment bank. Ten years ago, she and her two friends organized a semi-public swap party where people could exchange things they no longer needed. The party turned out to be a great success. Later they started the online swap platform, Jupyeah, meaning “making stuff” in Cantonese.



Samathy Woo, the CFO of Jupyeah, wears a dress she picked from Jupyeah’s swap event. Photo: Liu Xiaoqian Lisa

They also hold offline swapping events from time to time. Participants may bring the things they no longer need to the event and take preloved things from other people freely.


As the CFO and one of the event coordinators at Jupyeah, Samathy witnesses how many clothes Hong Kong people have dumped over the years.


She remembers there was one time they finished the Jupyeah event and returned to the warehouse. She saw many clothes and shoes there. “When the event ended, there were more than 20 bags of stuff left. The biggest IKEA bag, you know, has more than 20 bags. (Only) clothes.” Samathy recalled, “When I saw those clothes, I was so depressed.” “Why are there so many clothes? I was so annoyed.” When she went back home, she started to clean the closet.


In the beginning, her closet was “too stuffed to put another piece of clothes in there.” It took her two days to clean the wardrobe. During the cleaning process, she found that some of the clothes she had never tried and others did not fit anymore. “There are only several clothes I like to wear. Then I always think I don’t have enough clothes and then go shopping. Then not wearing (those clothes),” said Samathy.


She then took the clothes she no longer needed to the next Jupyeah event for other people to choose from. Now she only keeps the necessary clothes in the closet. Sometimes, she would also pick items from the event. “The dress I am wearing today is from one of Jupyeah’s events,” said Samathy.



The large-size IKEA bag Samathy mentioned. Photo: Liu Xiaoqian Lisa



Overconsumption of clothes in Hong Kong


Data shows that Hong Kong people buy more clothes than they need. As the survey from Redress in 2020 reports, two in five people in Hong Kong hold their clothes no longer than a year. Nearly one-third of the clothes in Hong Kong people’s closets are never or rarely worn. The rate almost doubled from 16% as suggested in a research from Greenpeace in 2016.




Ms. Fok just graduated from university. She has 15 to 20 sets of clothes that she wears frequently. “For the whole wardrobe, I think there are more than 30 sets of clothes. But for some of them, I do not think of wearing it.” She said.


Ellie Zhuang came to Hong Kong around seven years ago. She admits that many of her clothes are left in her wardrobe. “Probably just put it there and not take it out nor wear it for two years.” She said.


Wrong style, impulse purchase, and forgetting of having the clothes are the three most common reasons people leave their clothes unused, according to the Redress report.


“Our study shows that in Hong Kong, people's clothing behaviors are not at all aligned with the United Nations’ Sustainable Development Goal 12: Responsible Consumption and Production. With fashion's environmental impact set to drastically worsen, our results prove that Hong Kong has a long way to go towards steering a more sustainable fashion industry,” said Christina Dean, Founder and Chair of Redress.



Environmental Concerns


According to Redress, 61% of Hong Kongers give away their unwanted clothes to organizations that can recycle them, and 30% of respondents throw away their clothes into the bins.



However, not every piece of clothes can be recycled or reused. “Clothes that only use one material or can easily be taken apart can be recycled,” said Maxi Bohn, a senior product development expert who has over ten years of experience in the retail industry.


According to Redress, if the clothes are moldy, possibly contaminated with other items, in poor condition (e.g., broken), or the material is not recyclable, they would send it to the landfill. Around 1-3% of items they received, including actual rubbish such as masks, tissues, food wrappers, etc., end up in landfills.


According to the Environmental Protection Department (EPD) in Hong Kong, more than 95 thousand tonnes of textile waste is generated in Hong Kong every year. Only 7.6 percent of them are recycled. This is lower than the rate in the US three years ago. Around 242 tonnes of textile waste end up in landfills every day. That is the weight of 2.5 million t-shirts.




Burying the clothes under the soil brings environmental concerns. Synthetic materials such as polyester, acrylic, and nylon make up around 60% of the clothing material worldwide. Current technology cannot fully recycle these materials. If buried in soil, polyester may take more than 100 years before it completely biodegrade. As for nylon, which is made from crude oil and is commonly used to produce swimwear and activewear, it may take 30 to 40 years to decompose.

Circular Fashion offers a way out.

Yen is the owner of a break 93, a second-hand clothes store at Causeway Bay in Hong Kong. She described herself as a “shopaholic” when she was young.

“I love fashion. But at the same time I was struggling. Because loving fashion means destroying the earth. I find mit very contradictory."
— Yen, the owner of a break 93

She struggled to find a balance between pursuing fashion and protecting the environment. "Then I thought if there were any ways to make the best of both.” She could not find a secondhand clothes shop that met her expectation back then.“Because they were not pretty.” She said.


She then looked for ways to pursue fashion and also able to protect the environment at the same time. In 2017, she opened a break 93, adopting and reselling preloved apparel in Hong Kong. During the first two years of her business, 70 percent of the clothes sold at a break 93 were from herself.


Yen, the owner of a break 93. Photo: Liu Xiaoqian Lisa


The circular model plays a crucial role in fashion decarbonization. It can help extend product life, assist recycling and reduce the need to produce new clothes. It is estimated that the circular business model, including resale, sharing economy, repairment, can help the fashion industry cut around 143 million tonnes of greenhouse gas emissions in 2030.


Green Ladies & Green Little, one of the most well-known social eco enterprises in Hong Kong, is the first locel eco enterprise that adopts the consignment model for the secondhand clothes business. Consignors may take their unwanted clothes to Green Ladies’ shop. Before giving the clothes to the Green Ladies, consignors should clean the clothes. The staff will then check if the clothes meet the requirements (style, quality, and condition). If so, Green Ladies will display the clothes in the store for up to two months. If no one buys it after two months, Green Ladies will use the clothes for other purposes.


“For example, we collaborate with The Mills. They got some art exhibitions occasionally. So sometimes they will ask us to reserve some of the clothing to help them to create the artwork piece (s).” said Petty Lee, the project manager of Green Ladies & Green Little St. James' Settlement.


Overall performance of Green Ladies & Green Little. Picture provided by Petty Lee.


They would also donate unsold clothes to some international schools for teaching activities. “They just send us an email and telling us that they would like to have some clothing for their new syllabus and to let those students use it as the materials to like upcycle the clothing and redesign the clothing for the sustainable week,” She said.




Green Ladies & Green Little also sell secondhand shoes in the store. Photo: Liu Xiaoqian Lisa

Jupyeah also collaborates with The Mills for small events. Also, at the end of August, Jupyeah held its first offline event in 2021 in collaboration with Hysan Place. Hysan Place offered to fund that event. “At the beginning, no one finds us (for collaboration). Because no one knew us,” Samathy recalled, “I think it was after two to three years, the government found us for cooperation.” After then, Jupyeah began to receive invitations from private companies to organize workshops for the companies from time to time. “Social media is very important. If you are famous, many companies will come to you.” She said.


However, she also suggested that it is not efficient to recycle the massive amount of secondhand clothes by holding offline swap events periodically. “The impact is not very big.” She said. Since it takes a lot of time and energy to organize an event, it is also impossible to regularly hold offline events. Jupyeah now also runs its clothes recycling business. People may pay Jupyeah to pick up the clothes they no longer need. Jupyeah would then take pictures of the clothes and put them on their website for people to choose from.





The secondhand market is booming.

In the thredUP 2021 Resale Report, the secondhand market is estimated to double, valuing around HK$600 billion in 2025. Apart from traditional thrifting and donation, resale is the primary driver for the growth.




Among the thrifters, over 40% are GenZ and millennials. More than half of them claimed to send more money on pre-owned clothes in the next five years.


“A growing trend among consumers, especially the younger generation, has been to move away from permanent ownership of old clothing and look for sustainability and affordability

“It has a huge market,” “Many of the clothes in the closet are not being worn.”
— Samathy Woo, CFO at Jupyeah
fulfilled by rental and resale services. This also enables them to refresh their wardrobes. Hence, clothing remains the most preferred category in the rental and resale market.” said Ankita Roy, Retail Analyst at GlobalData.


Consumers also become more environmentally conscious during COVID-19. As people spend longer time at home, they become more sensitive to the surrounding environment and more aware of how much trash they generate every day. In a report from Accenture in 2020, ​​45% of consumers admitted that they consider sustainability when making purchasing decisions and tend to cultivate this as a habit in the future.


Apart from environmental concerns, saving money is another reason why consumers choose secondhand items. The recent research from Carousell, one of the most popular online marketplace in South Asia, suggests that more than half of the Hong Kong respondents have bought secondhand items before to save money.




More people are accepting the concept in Hong Kong.

According to Redress, 31% of Hong Kongers have already owned “secondhand” clothing by 2020. Carousell found that on average Hong Kong people have 43 unused items at home that can be sold.


At Jupyeah, the most popular good is female clothes. “So many girls come (to the event). Everyone takes a bag of clothes,” Samathy said. “Jupyeah has many fans,” “Some come to the event every time.”


Jupyeah's 10th Anniversary Fashion Pop-up Swap on December 4th 2021. Photo: Liu Xiaoqian Lisa


But it was not the case ten years ago. Many people did not accept the idea. They could not understand why they needed to pay in order to join the swapping event and to get secondhand clothes. “Why wear secondhand clothes?” Samathy recalled some people would ask.


“At the beginning, it was more because of the greediness,” Samathy recalled. “There was a time when we had a Jupyeah event in the old days, some aunties brought a suitcase with them.” “Regardless of what was what, they took everything.” She said. Now attendants are not allowed to bring suitcases to the event. Jupyeah provides large IKEA bags for attendants to carry their stuff. Each person can take up to one IKEA bag of things at the event.


“It was after four to five years since we started doing this thing that people began to change their mind.” She said. “There are more people who accept secondhand clothes across Hong Kong nowadays, I think.”


When asked what brings the changes, she referred to the general environment in the world. “The trend of sustainability, climate change, pollution. These topics about environmental protection,” said Samathy, “Those promotions help.” She also mentioned that social media plays a role in increasing people’s awareness of sustainability, sharing, and secondhand consumption.

COVID-19 brings unprecedented challenges.

The COVID-19 has brought unprecedented challenges to Yen’s business. Since the pandemic began, there have been fewer visitors coming to the physical shop. People have been working at home and reducing the frequency of going out. “There has been little demand for clothes.” She said, “The pandemic is the most serious impact we have encountered since we started the business.”


For the supply side, Yen also met some problems. “The quality of the clothes (received from customers) declined,” she said.


A break 93 adopts pre-loved apparel from customers and resells the items in the store. It only accepts female clothes or bags at least 80 percent new. “Most of the consignors are from the middle class,” said Yen, “Before the pandemic, they would travel to Japan, to Europe, and buy clothes. If they found the clothes did not fit, they would bring it here and sell it.” However, travel has been restricted since the pandemic began. She found that that group of consignors had disappeared. “Because they didn’t buy new clothes, we didn’t (receive many) new clothes during that time.”


She also notices that as people purchase fewer clothes, instead of selling their old clothes, they would wear the old clothes repeatedly, leaving them few clothes to send to a break 93 for sale.


Redress also found that people re-wear the same clothes more often than pre-COVID times. According to the survey, two out of three Hong Kongers have changed their clothes consumption habits due to COVID-19, and more than half of the respondents said they had enough clothes.


Ms. Fok admits that she now buys fewer clothes than in the pre-pandemic period. “After the pandemic, I do go out of the street much less (than before).” She said. For example, she would go out for up to twenty-five days per month before COVID-19 but only five days during the pandemic, including going downstairs to fetch the take-out.


But Ellie finds herself buying more clothes than before. Before the pandemic, she used to buy clothes one to two times a month. She mentioned that because now she travels less and spends more time in Hong Kong, she can buy more clothes than before. “Around two to three times a month,” said Ellie.


Due to the social movement and pandemic, Jupyeah has not holden any events for nearly two years. During that time, they had no income. Samathy said that they still needed to pay the fixed cost. “For example, we have storage. We need to pay the warehouse.”


Green Ladies & Green Little also faced a downturn in its secondhand clothes business during the past year. According to Petty, the enterprise witnessed around a 25% drop in its sales at the beginning of the pandemic.


Human resource dilemma

The other challenge is people management.


“People are terrified of the virus. Our staff also. They just worried, and they don't want to take risks to work and to like serving some strangers,” Petty recalled, “And also at the beginning, some of the customers were not quite used to wearing the mask. They even came to our shop without wearing their masks. So it was very difficult for us to comfort our stuff and to be more professional.” During the Chinese New Year in 2020, when the COVID-19 broke out in Hong Kong, Green Ladies closed its stores for two weeks to let the staff rest at home and minimize the risk of spreading the virus.


The staff at Green Ladies & Green Little take the consigned clothes out off the bag. Photo: Liu Xiaoqian Lisa


The situation is much better now as people are getting used to the pandemic. “Our staff also feel more confident because they know more about the virus thing and how to prevent it,” Petty said.


But things are different for Yen.


“Recruitment was easier during the pandemic,” Yen said. She was looking for part-time shop assistants. “Due to the economic recession during the pandemic, many people could not land a full-time job, especially fresh graduates. They choose to do a part-time job first.”


But it is no longer the case now. As the economy in Hong Kong is recovering from the pandemic, it becomes less challenging for people to find a full-time job. “After July in 2021, people receive full-time offers gradually,” said Yen. It is hard for a break 93 to maintain its staff during the post-pandemic era. “Everyone wants a full-time job.” She said. “The pay here is not very high. Most of our employees were here for transitions (to a full-time job).”


A break 93 opened a pop-up shop at Prince Edward during the pandemic because of cheap rent. But Yen had to close the shop after eight months. “Because we couldn’t find people to hire.” She said.



Hygiene Concern?

Clothes and towels can carry germs and viruses. The secondhand clothing stores interviewed have given special care to the hygiene of their clothes during the pandemic but not much. This is partly because usually, the clothes need to wait for several days before showing up in the store. Research suggests that coronavirus can remain on the fabric surface for up to 72 hours.


“When we collect those clothing, we need some more time to handle it for the data entry and inventory issue. We will keep the clothes say more than ten days before showing them at our shop,” Petty explained. Green Ladies also purchases a particular sanitizer specializing in tackling the coronavirus. The staff would use the spray to sanitize the clothes after customers try them on.


Consignors must clean the clothes before giving them to the secondhand shops. Both Green Ladies and a break 93 do not wash the clothes they received but would steam the clothes before displaying them in the store.




The staff at Green Ladirs & Green Little steams the clothes before displaying it in the store. gif: Liu Xiaoqian Lisa

“First of all, we will suggest our consignor clean their clothes before giving them to us,” said Petty. “I think it is an educational process because we would like to educate them to donate or consign more consciously before giving us what we can call their waste or something extra.”


Yen also mentioned that secondhand clothes are actually “cleaner” than the new ones because there are many chemical leftovers from the manufacturing process on the new clothes, which may cause harm to our skin. For example, disperse dye, a synthetic dye used in clothes production, may cause allergic contact dermatitis. Expert suggests that this chemical is present in a high volume on new clothes. It is also mentioned that washing helps remove this chemical from the clothes.


“But for the secondhand, the person (previous owner) has washed and worn many times. Those chemicals are washed away. Your skin will not be so sensitive (to the chemicals).” She said.



Sustainability is not the only drive. Fashion also matters.

Most NGOs or secondhand shops in Hong Kong would emphasize the importance of sustainability in their brands. But from Yen’s point of view, it might not be the case.


“I would not abandon my good life for sustainability,” said she. Before starting a break 93, Yen had decided to make fashion the top priority of the business. “People look for beautiful things.”


“If you want to make this model a sustainable business model, you must take different needs from different people in the market into consideration.”
— Yen, the owner of a break 93

Staff at a break 93 offer to help customers choose clothes and find their styles at the store. “Many customers come here because they know the clothes here are pretty. Also, they know how to get a style,” said Yen.


She admits that there is a group of people who shops for sustainability. “They themselves are very eco-friendly.” “But there is also a group of people in the market who do not buy (the concept) at all. That is the group of people who love fashion so much.” Yen said. She mentioned that it is hard to persuade the fashion lovers to buy secondhand for sustainability as they wonder why they would buy secondhand clothes if it look bad?

Some people criticize a break 93 for being not environmental-friendly enough. “Sometimes people may wonder why many of their clothes are not accepted (by a break 93) even there is only a little flaw. You say you are eco-friendly but you don’t take my clothes.” Yen recalled. “We would explain that the sense of fashion, cleanness, and completeness is so important to us.”




A picture at a break 93 shows how to pick the style. Photo: Liu Xiaoqian Lisa


Who are the consumers?

Apart from fashion lovers, Yen also mentioned that people come to a break 93 to find new clothes when they change their jobs. “They may not know what to wear. Then they come to choose (clothes).” Pregnant women also come to find their clothes. “Their belly would get bigger and bigger and then return to small. Buying new clothes would be wasteful. So they would choose secondhand clothes, which are cheaper and save them from creating new trash.” said Yen.


She has seen customers aged between 25 and 60 years old coming to the shop. “Most of them are from 30 to 45 years old.” She said. “Some students would come. Some customers would take their mom here.”.




Two female customers at a break 93 on Thursday, October 21st 2021. Photo: Liu Xiaoqian Lisa

Many of the Jupyeah’s events participants are in their 20s and 30s. Samathy said it is a good sign that many young people accept using secondhand goods nowadays. “If they accept it, their next-generation would also accept it.”


While more and more people are trying secondhand clothes in Hong Kong, others may not give too much thought about it. Ellie said she had come across vintage clothes on the internet and did not hate secondhand clothes, but she did not know where to buy them. “I don’t have any people around me have the hobby (of buying secondhand clothes),” said she. Ms. Fok has never purchased pre-owned clothes. “I think I am a little bit of mysophobia. I prefer to wear brand new clothes,” She said.




Sarah Garner is the Founder of Retykle, an online platform for people to buy and sell pre-loved designer children’s & maternity fashion. In the documentary reFashioned, she also talked about parents’ concerns about buying secondhand clothes. “The Chinese are particularly skeptical about secondhand children’s clothing because they don’t want to pass on bad luck from the previous owner.”


“Actually, many people told us that they want to consign and sell men’s clothes or children’s clothes,” said Yen, “But I think it is just a tiny part of the whole.” She mentioned that it is not because many mothers do not like secondhand clothing, but they just really love their babies and would rather buy new clothes. “Some people may say they would exchange clothes with their friends.” she said. But buying secondhand clothes from a shop is different from exchanging clothes with someone you know. “Because you know where the clothes come from if your friend gives it to you.” She explained.


Secondhand children’s clothes displayed at Retykle's pop-up store in November 2021. Photo: Liu Xiaoqian Lisa


When asked to rate the level of awareness of sustainable fashion consumption in Hong Kong, Petty thinks Hong Kong is at 60% of Japan. She mentioned that she found secondhand clothes were quite popular when she visited Japan. “It is like a treasure,” said Petty. “You can see the secondhand clothing, secondhand shops everywhere.”


“Maybe not many outlets or many shops are doing the same thing in Hong Kong. So I would say like 60 % of Japan.” She said.

A mirror in Retykle's shop with a slogan printed on it. Photo: Photo: Liu Xiaoqian Lisa


“Protect the earth. Stay fashion.”

For the future, Jupyeah is thinking about installing a reg at the clothes shops in Hong Kong. The shops can buy secondhand clothes from Jupyeah at a very low price and resell them in the store.



The reg from Jupyeah at Slowood. Source: Slowood Facebook page


“We hope...there will be a part in the store selling clothes supplied by Jupyeah,” said Samathy, “If many small shops are willing to help us do this, then we can deal with more secondhand clothes.” She said. “The goal is to (let consumers) have a choice. You can choose to buy new clothes, but you can also choose to buy second-hand ones. It is also very new.” She hopes that Jupyeah will promote the secondhand clothes business to more retailers in Hong Kong. They have once collaborated with Slowood, an eco-friendly commodity shop in Hong Kong.





“Even if you do not collaborate with us, we also hope that you can sell the secondhand clothes of your brand in your store. This would also be a great contribution to sustainability,” said Samathy.


Yen also believes that there should be secondhand clothes stores across all the regions in Hong Kong. “Therefore, residents from every region can enjoy high-quality secondhand (clothes).”
